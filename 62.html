<h2>About Me</h2>

<p>I am Rui Zhu, a postdoctoral scholar at the <a target="_blank" href="http://spatial.ucsb.edu/">Center for Spatial Studies, <a target="_blank" href="https://geog.ucsb.edu/">Department of Geography</a>, <a target="_blank" href="https://www.ucsb.edu/">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>. My research interests include geospatial semantics, spatial statistics, as well as their broader interactions in geospatial artificial intelligence (Geo-AI). More concretely, I combine theory-informed approach and data-driven appraoch to address geospatial challenges such as geospatial data interoperability, spatial predictions, qualitative spatial reasoning, and so on. My work has been applied to a wide range of applications, indluding urban studies, global health, environmental modeling, as well as humanitarian aids. I am currently working on the <a target="_blank" href="https://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/showAward?AWD_ID=2033521&HistoricalAwards=false">KnowWhereGraph (KWG) project</a> funded by <a target="_blank" href="https://beta.nsf.gov/funding/initiatives/convergence-accelerator">NSF Convergence Accelerator Program</a>.
</p>

<p>I obtained my Ph.D. degree from the <a target="_blank" href="https://www.ucsb.edu/">University of California, Santa Barbara</a> in 2020 under the advise of <a target="_blank" href="https://geog.ucsb.edu/~jano/">Dr. Krzysztof Janowicz</a> and <a target="_blank" href="https://www.cut.ac.cy/faculties/fet/ceg/staff/phaedon.kyriakidis/?languageId=1">Dr. Phaedon Kyriakidis</a>. I hold a M.S. degree in Information Sciences from the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.pitt.edu/">University of Pittsburgh</a> working with <a target="_blank" href="http://gis.sis.pitt.edu/">Dr. Hassan Karimi</a>.

</p>

<div class="project-item-content">
<h3><strong>Geo-Multipole: A High-order Geographic Conceptualization</strong></h3>

<p>Conventional spatial statistics are based on first- or second-order stationarity/statistics. However, due to the complex nature of geographic phenomena, their spatial patterns are frequently beyond simple low-order statistics. Therefore, this project proposed a new conceptualization named as Geo-multipole to model higher-order spatial interactions. A specific approach, multiple-point geostatistics, was employed as one example of Geo-multipole to generate complex urban structures from remotely sensed images. <a href="http://geog.ucsb.edu/~zhu/papers/GeoMultipole2017_ZhuKyriakidisJanowicz.pdf">Related paper</a></p>

<h3><strong>Automatic Selection of Landmarks</strong></h3>

<p>In this research, several supervised learning models were applied to select “salient” objects as landmarks. Features were extracted from various sources, including traditional databases, social networks and LiDAR data. The features did not only represent static aspects of the object, but also the interactions between objects and humans. <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/tgis.12095">Related paper</a></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>

<div class="project-item-content">
<h3><strong>Personalized Accessibility Map (PAM)</strong></h3>

<p>Personalized Accessible Maps is a project focusing on providing navigation services to a wide range of users on campus. The prototype of this project was implemented at the University of Pittsburgh. The service provided three kinds of route for different users, including shuttle path, shortest path and personalized path. For personalized path, factors like slope, traffic, and width of the road were considered. In terms of navigation guidance, both turn-by-turn and landmark-based instructions were implemented in this service. Different POIs, service areas, accessible entrances and sidewalks were demonstrated on the maps as well. I was one of the developers for this project. <a href="https://ischoolatpitt.com/2014/04/10/personalized-accessibility-map-2/">Link</a></p>
</div>
</div>
</div>

<div class="project-item">

</div>
<div class="project-item-content">
<h3><strong>Pedestrian Navigation Services (PNS)</strong></h3>

<p>PNS aims to provide navigation guidance for pedestrians. In this service, the sidewalk, rather than road networks, was utilized to generate the route. Due to the smaller granularity of pedestrian navigation compared to vehicle navigation, more challenges were imposed on the techniques of map matching. It required the results of map matching to be highly accurate. To overcome this issue, a new algorithm, chain-code map matching, was implemented in this system. This technique largely improved the accuracy of positioning pedestrians. <a href="http://www.nrcresearchpress.com/doi/pdf/10.5623/cig2013-052">Relevant paper</a></p>
</div>
